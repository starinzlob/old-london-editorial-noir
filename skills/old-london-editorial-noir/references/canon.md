# Old London Editorial Noir Canon

## Definition

Old London Editorial Noir is not a generic vintage filter. It combines:

- 1920s–1930s London and interwar British visual culture
- British newspapers, classified ads, commercial handbills, and private-press imprints
- Victorian, Edwardian, Gothic, railway, and industrial architecture
- film-noir fog, rain, gaslight, warm darkness, and quiet suspense
- letterpress, coarse halftone, screen print, etching, and wood-engraving imperfections
- restrained Swiss-grid discipline, strong editorial hierarchy, negative space, and modern legibility

The result should feel like a carefully preserved private London newspaper: historical and tactile, but still clear enough to read and useful enough to live with.

## Emotional register

Aim for quiet, mysterious, melancholic, intimate, observant, restrained, warm-dark, slow, cinematic, and timeless.

- Build mystery through absence, framing, partial occlusion, light, shadow, and pacing.
- Prefer the feeling of reading an old paper, book, case file, or private archive to theatrical costume drama.
- Let human traces and printing accidents remain, while keeping the composition exact.
- Avoid horror shorthand, gothic excess, luxurious nostalgia, or flamboyant “period spectacle.”

## Visual motifs

### City and architecture

- narrow London lanes, Baker Street atmosphere, cobbles, railway platforms, clocks, brick and stone façades
- Victorian and Edwardian shopfronts, Gothic details, industrial structures, wet reflections, lamps in fog
- newspaper photography, architectural engraving, etched plates, or coarse printed reproductions

Use place as evidence and atmosphere, not as a checklist of landmarks.

### Objects

- typewriter, pocket watch, brass fitting, magnifier, fountain pen, ink, old book, map
- wax seal, suitcase, correspondence, precision mechanisms, archive labels

Treat an object as a narrative clue. Do not assemble a dense “vintage prop pack.”

### Printer’s mark

When a personal mark is appropriate, use a knot, a restrained wheat trace, and a lucky-star negative shape:

- draw it as a monochrome printer’s ornament or private-press colophon
- let the star emerge from line or negative space instead of attaching a badge
- reduce wheat to a subtle terminal or texture, never a full wreath
- keep it legible at small size

Avoid red five-point stars, wheat wreaths, gears, radial rays, medals, perfect emblem symmetry, and Soviet political associations.

## Palette

| Role | Color | Typical use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Cool gray newsprint | `#D8D3C7` | primary paper field |
| Cream paper | `#E7DDC6` | warm archive, advert, or letter |
| Ivory | `#F0E9DA` | highlight paper and reversed type |
| Charcoal | `#1E211D` | primary ink, type, structure |
| Faded ink | `#4F5047` | secondary information |
| Deep burgundy | `#762F32` | rare title, state, seal, or registration accent |
| Forest green | `#294438` | quiet classification or tool accent |
| Brass | `#A68C59` | tiny material highlight only |
| Deep brown | `#3A2E24` | wood, leather, and selected shadows |

Rules:

- Default to charcoal on cool gray newsprint.
- Use cream, burgundy, forest green, brass, and brown as limited supporting inks or materials.
- Keep saturation slightly faded while maintaining sufficient contrast.
- Use parchment only for a narratively justified letter, archive, or advertisement.
- Never solve the style with one brown/yellow filter.
- Exclude neon, electric blue, vivid purple, cyber cyan, rainbow gradients, plastic highlights, and large gold gradients.

## Typography

### English

- Masthead only: blackletter or historical newspaper wordmark, such as UnifrakturCook.
- Headlines: Newsreader, Cheltenham, Caslon, Baskerville, or a similarly weighty editorial serif.
- Navigation, issue data, bylines, and figures: Gill Sans, Johnston, or restrained humanist sans.
- Advertising: Clarendon or a compact slab/display face, used as one controlled voice.

### Chinese

- Prefer a modern Song/Ming serif such as Source Han Serif or Songti SC for headlines and body.
- Do not imitate blackletter in Chinese.
- Build the relationship through weight, rule lines, size, spacing, and hierarchy.
- When English leads and Chinese supports, keep the Chinese subtitle smaller and calmer.

### Rules

- Let the masthead be expressive; keep article typography controlled.
- Use rules, double rules, columns, date, place, issue, price, and byline to establish newspaper identity.
- Use uppercase only for short labels and rubrics.
- Keep tracking neutral by default; do not rely on negative tracking.
- Use editable type for copy that must be correct, searchable, or accessible.

## Composition

- Establish one lead story or focal image, then supporting evidence.
- Use editorial grids, narrow measures, asymmetric spans, rule lines, inset notices, and deliberate blank paper.
- Prefer square or nearly square corners and printed frames to floating cards or pills.
- Keep reading order unmistakable even when the layout crosses columns.
- Let texture and ornament support hierarchy rather than compete with it.
- On small frames, simplify to one or two columns and protect title/image separation.

For a single generated image, translate these ideas into focal hierarchy, controlled margins, asymmetry, crop, and negative space rather than simulating an entire illegible newspaper page.

## Image material

- Make coarse halftone dots visible where appropriate.
- Use localized ink bleed, ink gain, incomplete coverage, slight misregistration, plate scratches, folds, or show-through.
- Keep defects uneven and physically plausible.
- Preserve recognizable faces, architecture, objects, and gestures.
- Favor raw cool-gray newsprint over decorative yellow parchment.
- Combine photo and print treatment intentionally: a real photographic value structure can survive beneath halftone and ink.

## Motion character

- Motion is measured, mechanical, observational, and editorial.
- Favor restrained push-ins, lateral rostrum moves, page reveals, registration slips, shutters, wipes motivated by rules, and slow parallax.
- Let stillness carry tension. Do not animate every layer.
- Use fog, rain, paper, ink, and light as subtle living systems rather than particle-show effects.
- Cut on ideas, glances, type impacts, page turns, or sound cues.
- Keep transitions brief and legible; avoid template-like glitch, zoom, spin, bounce, and elastic UI motion.

## Hard negatives

Reject:

- generic sepia vintage templates
- cyberpunk, futuristic UI, glassmorphism, neon, glossy 3D, plastic surfaces
- purple-blue technology palettes and decorative gradients
- modern SaaS card walls, giant radii, pill navigation, glowing blobs
- cluttered steampunk gears, brass pipes, steam machines
- uniform noise, blur, vignette, or darkness that destroys readability
- Soviet propaganda, political emblems, red-star-and-wheat compositions
- generated images carrying dynamic body copy, functional controls, or accessibility-critical text

## Canonical prompt vocabulary

Use only the terms that serve the subject:

```text
interwar British private-press editorial, 1920s–1930s London,
coarse visible halftone dots, letterpress ink gain, low-fidelity newsprint,
localized ink bleed and slight plate misregistration, architectural engraving,
cool gray raw newspaper stock, charcoal-black ink, restrained burgundy accent,
quiet film-noir atmosphere, fog, rain, gaslight, clear editorial hierarchy,
highly legible subject, authentic commercial newspaper illustration
```

Common negative vocabulary:

```text
generic sepia wash, neon, cyberpunk, futuristic UI, glossy 3D, plastic,
smooth vector art, clean digital gradient, purple-blue tech palette,
cartoon, anime, Y2K, steampunk clutter, uniform grunge overlay,
Soviet emblem, red-star badge, wheat wreath, propaganda poster
```
